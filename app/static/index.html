📁 Load Tesla privacy / usage data

Request your data from Tesla (tesla.com → Privacy → Download Your Data). Tesla's ZIP is password-protected, so unzip it first and select the CSVs inside — you can pick several at once (e.g. “Charging Data.csv” + “Vehicle Details.csv”) and they'll be merged. CSV / JSON accepted.

⬆️ Drop a file here or browse
Expected columns

Drives: start_time, end_time, distance (km or miles), duration, start_soc, end_soc, energy_used, avg_speed, outside_temp, start/end_location.

Charges: start_time, end_time, energy_added, charge_type (AC/DC), max_power, start_soc, end_soc, location, cost.

Column names are matched loosely (case / spacing insensitive).

🚀 First-time setup guide

How to run your own copy of Tesla Analyzer and connect your real car — so it logs every drive and charge automatically. Everything below is free and can be done entirely from your iPhone in about 20 minutes. You only do this once.

Your Tesla → Tesla's API → your copy of this app (hosted on Render) → history saved in a database (Neon) → you view it here.

💡 Just want to look around first? You don't need any of this to try the app. Ask Tesla for your data (tesla.com → Privacy → Download Your Data) and use 📁 Load Tesla Data — it analyses on your phone, no hosting needed. The steps below are only for automatic, always-on logging.

  1. 1 🍴 Fork the code to your own GitHub

    Why: Render builds the app from a GitHub repository. Forking makes your own copy of the code under your account, so your deployment is fully independent — it keeps working no matter what happens to the original.

    1. You'll need a free GitHub account — the same login also signs you into Render and Neon later. Open github.com → Sign up.
    2. Open the Tesla Analyzer repository and tap Fork (top-right) → Create fork. That copies it into your GitHub.
    3. Done — you now own a copy at github.com/<you>/Tesla-Analyzer. You'll point Render at this fork in Step 3.

    🔄 Keeping your copy up to date. A fork is a snapshot — it doesn't change on its own when the original app is improved. To pull in the latest version, open your fork on GitHub and tap Sync fork → Update branch (one click). Because Render is connected to your fork, it then redeploys automatically. Your logged data in Neon is untouched by an update, so it's safe to do anytime.

    🤖 Prefer fully hands-off updates? Your fork includes a ready-made “Sync fork with upstream” action. Just enable it once: open your fork's Actions tab → “I understand… enable them”. From then on it checks hourly and auto-syncs your fork whenever the original changes — and Render redeploys on its own. No clicks needed after that.

  2. 2 🗄️ Neon — a free database that never expires

    Why: the free Render server (Step 3) forgets everything each time it sleeps. Neon stores your drives and charges permanently — its free tier doesn't expire after 30 days like most others.

    1. Open neon.tech → Sign up with GitHub.
    2. Create a new project (any name, pick the region closest to you).
    3. Copy the connection string — it starts with postgresql://…. Keep it handy for Step 3.
  3. 3 ☁️ Render — host your own copy

    Why: this puts the app on the internet so your phone can open it and it can talk to Tesla. The free plan sleeps after ~15 min idle — that's the brief “reloading / spinning” screen you sometimes see; it wakes again in about 30–60 seconds.

    1. Open render.com → Sign up with GitHub (use the account you forked with in Step 1).
    2. New → Blueprint, then choose your fork (<you>/Tesla-Analyzer). Render reads the included render.yaml automatically.
    3. Render will prompt you to fill a few secrets:
      • DATABASE_URL — paste your Neon string from Step 2.
      • APP_PASSCODE — any passcode you like; the app asks for it so only you can open your dashboard.
      • SYNC_KEY — any secret word; used for auto-logging (Step 6).
      • TESLA_CLIENT_ID / TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET — leave blank for now; you'll get these in Step 4 and add them after.
    4. Deploy. You'll get a public address like https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com. Note it down — you'll reuse it in Step 4.
  4. 4 🔑 Tesla Developer account — permission to read your car

    Why: Tesla only lets an officially-registered app use the “Sign in with Tesla” button. This step creates that registration.

    1. Open developer.tesla.com and sign in with your normal Tesla account. Create a new App.
    2. Fill the app details using your Render address from Step 3:
      • Allowed Origin: https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com
      • Redirect / Callback URI: https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com/api/link/oauth/callback
      • Scopes — tick these permissions (see the note below for what each one does).
    3. Copy the Client ID and Client Secret. In Render, open your service → Environment, paste them into TESLA_CLIENT_ID / TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET, and let it redeploy.

    🔓 Which scopes to tick. In the developer portal's permission list, enable these — they're what the app actually uses:

    • Vehicle Information vehicle_device_data — required. Reads drives, battery level, range and charge data — the core of every chart and KPI.
    • Vehicle Location vehicle_location — recommended. Gives trip start/end places and live speed. Skip it and the app still works, but locations show as blank/coordinates.
    • Vehicle Commands vehicle_cmds — recommended. Lets ⟳ Sync gently wake a sleeping car so it can report fresh data. Without it, sync only sees the car when it's already awake.
    • You can leave the rest off — Vehicle Charging Management, Energy Products, Profile Information, etc. are not used.

    📝 Don't see TESLA_CLIENT_ID / TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET in the list? If you left them blank in Step 3, Render won't have created the rows. Just add them yourself: tap + Add Environment Variable, type the key name exactly (TESLA_CLIENT_ID, then again for TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET), paste each value, and Save changes — it redeploys automatically.

    ✅ Public key / domain check is automatic. Tesla verifies your domain by fetching a key file — this app already serves it at /.well-known/appspecific/com.tesla.3p.public-key.pem, and the first sign-in registers your domain for you. Nothing to do here.

  5. 5 🔗 Link & sign in — inside this app
    1. Open your Render address and enter your APP_PASSCODE.
    2. Tap 🔗 Link Tesla Account → Sign in with Tesla, then approve the request on Tesla's page.
    3. Done — the header badge turns green “live”. Tap ⟳ Sync anytime to wake the car and pull the latest.

    🚗🚗 Have two or more cars on one Tesla account? You still sign in just once — a single Tesla login covers every car on that account. The app finds them all automatically and shows each as a card on the main page; tap one to open its dashboard, and use ← Main to switch. Each car keeps its own separate trips, charging and battery history. There's nothing extra to set up per car.

    🔀 Cars on different Tesla accounts? The app follows one account at a time. To switch accounts, use 🔌 Unlink / switch account on the main page, then Sign in with the other account. Your logged history is kept either way.

    👥 Sharing this link with someone? This is your own single dashboard, not a multi-user app. Anyone you give the Render address and the APP_PASSCODE to will see your car's real data — opening it never resets your Tesla login or reverts to demo. To let a friend just explore, send them the public demo instead (chuahph.github.io/Tesla-Analyzer) or have them use 📁 Load Tesla Data with their own Tesla export — both keep their data on their own device, with no access to your car. For their own live analytics, they'd set up their own copy (Steps 1–5).

  6. 6 🔄 Hands-off logging — recommended

    Why: because Render sleeps, a small scheduled “ping” keeps your history filling in even when you're not looking at the app. Pick one of these:

    🤖 All-in-GitHub (recommended). Your fork already includes a “Sync car from Tesla” action that pings every 5 minutes — no third-party site. Enable it once: open your fork's Actions tab and enable workflows, then add two repository secrets (Settings → Secrets and variables → Actions): RENDER_URL = your Render address, and SYNC_KEY = the same value from your Render environment. Done — it auto-syncs from GitHub.

    ⏰ Or a free scheduler. On cron-job.org, create a job that visits https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com/api/sync?key=YOUR_SYNC_KEY every 5 minutes (use the SYNC_KEY from Step 3).

    🔋 Every 5 minutes is safe. A built-in sleep window means that once the car has been idle ~15 min, the app stops actually polling it (the 5-minute pings just keep Render awake), so it won't cause vampire battery drain. The tighter interval simply catches your drives more often while you're actually moving — which gives accurate live speed (incl. max speed) instead of a trip reconstructed after the fact.

  7. 7 📲 Install on your iPhone
    1. Open your Render address in Safari.
    2. Tap the Share button (square with an ↑ arrow).
    3. Choose Add to Home Screen → Add. It now launches full-screen from a ⚡ icon, like a native app, and opens even when offline.

          🌡️ Efficiency vs Temperature

          Average Wh/km grouped by the outside temperature during each drive (10°C bands). Shows how weather drives consumption — cold raises it (cabin/battery heating) and high heat raises it too (A/C). Each bar is the mean Wh/km of that band's drives.

          📈 Weekly Efficiency Trend

          Your average Wh/km per ISO week across the window — the mean of each week's drives. Reveals seasonal drift and whether your efficiency is improving over time.

          🛣️ Distance by Speed Band

          Total km driven grouped by each trip's average speed — City (<30), Urban (30–60), Rural (60–90), Highway (90+ km/h). Shows where your mileage happens; highway km cost the most energy per km (aerodynamic drag rises with speed²).

          🕒 Trips by Hour of Day

          Number of trips that started in each hour (0–23, Malaysia time). Reveals your commute rhythm and how much driving falls in peak-hour traffic.

          ⚡ Charging: AC vs DC Energy

          Share of total kWh added by AC (home / destination) vs DC (Supercharger / fast). DC is quick but adds heat and cell stress and usually costs more per kWh — a lower DC share is gentler on the pack and your wallet.

          🎯 Charge Target (End SoC)

          How often each end-of-charge battery level is used (rounded to 5%). Frequent charges to 100% accelerate degradation; keeping daily charges to 80–90% and only topping to 100% before long trips extends pack life.

          💡 Recommendations to improve efficiency & battery health

          📍 Named places

          Give a spot a name (e.g. "Home", "Office") and any trip starting or ending within its radius shows that name instead of the geocoded address — including trips already logged.

            🔧 Service & Tyres

            Log maintenance you've had done — tyre rotations, filters, brake fluid — and this tracks when each is next due, from Tesla's own published intervals. Purely what you log here; the car doesn't report service history itself.
