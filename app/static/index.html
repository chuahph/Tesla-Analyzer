📁 Load Tesla privacy / usage data

Request your data from Tesla (tesla.com → Privacy → Download Your Data). Tesla's ZIP is password-protected, so unzip it first and select the CSVs inside — you can pick several at once (e.g. “Charging Data.csv” + “Vehicle Details.csv”) and they'll be merged. CSV / JSON accepted.

⬆️ Drop a file here or browse
Expected columns

Drives: start_time, end_time, distance (km or miles), duration, start_soc, end_soc, energy_used, avg_speed, outside_temp, start/end_location.

Charges: start_time, end_time, energy_added, charge_type (AC/DC), max_power, start_soc, end_soc, location, cost.

Column names are matched loosely (case / spacing insensitive).

🚀 First-time setup guide

How to run your own copy of Tesla Analyzer and connect your real car — so it logs every drive and charge automatically. Everything below is free and can be done entirely from your iPhone in about 20 minutes. You only do this once.

Your Tesla → Tesla's API → your copy of this app (hosted on Render) → history saved in a database (Neon) → you view it here.

💡 Just want to look around first? You don't need any of this to try the app. Ask Tesla for your data (tesla.com → Privacy → Download Your Data) and use 📁 Load Tesla Data — it analyses on your phone, no hosting needed. The steps below are only for automatic, always-on logging.

  1. 1 🗄️ Neon — a free database that never expires

    Why: the free Render server (Step 2) forgets everything each time it sleeps. Neon stores your drives and charges permanently — its free tier doesn't expire after 30 days like most others.

    1. Open neon.tech → Sign up with GitHub.
    2. Create a new project (any name, pick the region closest to you).
    3. Copy the connection string — it starts with postgresql://…. Keep it handy for Step 2.
  2. 2 ☁️ Render — host your own copy

    Why: this puts the app on the internet so your phone can open it and it can talk to Tesla. The free plan sleeps after ~15 min idle — that's the brief “reloading / spinning” screen you sometimes see; it wakes again in about 30–60 seconds.

    1. Open render.com → Sign up with GitHub.
    2. New → Blueprint, then choose the Tesla-Analyzer repository. Render reads the included render.yaml automatically.
    3. Render will prompt you to fill a few secrets:
      • DATABASE_URL — paste your Neon string from Step 1.
      • APP_PASSCODE — any passcode you like; the app asks for it so only you can open your dashboard.
      • SYNC_KEY — any secret word; used for auto-logging (Step 5).
      • TESLA_CLIENT_ID / TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET — leave blank for now; you'll get these in Step 3 and add them after.
    4. Deploy. You'll get a public address like https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com. Note it down — you'll reuse it in Step 3.
  3. 3 🔑 Tesla Developer account — permission to read your car

    Why: Tesla only lets an officially-registered app use the “Sign in with Tesla” button. This step creates that registration.

    1. Open developer.tesla.com and sign in with your normal Tesla account. Create a new App.
    2. Fill the app details using your Render address from Step 2:
      • Allowed Origin: https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com
      • Redirect / Callback URI: https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com/api/link/oauth/callback
      • Scopes: Vehicle Information & Vehicle Data (openid, offline_access, vehicle_device_data).
    3. Copy the Client ID and Client Secret. In Render, open your service → Environment, paste them into TESLA_CLIENT_ID / TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET, and let it redeploy.

    📝 Don't see TESLA_CLIENT_ID / TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET in the list? If you left them blank in Step 2, Render won't have created the rows. Just add them yourself: tap + Add Environment Variable, type the key name exactly (TESLA_CLIENT_ID, then again for TESLA_CLIENT_SECRET), paste each value, and Save changes — it redeploys automatically.

    ✅ Public key / domain check is automatic. Tesla verifies your domain by fetching a key file — this app already serves it at /.well-known/appspecific/com.tesla.3p.public-key.pem, and the first sign-in registers your domain for you. Nothing to do here.

  4. 4 🔗 Link & sign in — inside this app
    1. Open your Render address and enter your APP_PASSCODE.
    2. Tap 🔗 Link Tesla Account → Sign in with Tesla, then approve the request on Tesla's page.
    3. Done — the header badge turns green “live”. Tap ⟳ Sync anytime to wake the car and pull the latest.
  5. 5 🔄 Hands-off logging — recommended

    Why: because Render sleeps, a small scheduled “ping” keeps your history filling in even when you're not looking at the app.

    1. Open a free scheduler like cron-job.org.
    2. Create a job that visits https://ev-drive-analyzer.onrender.com/api/sync?key=YOUR_SYNC_KEY every 15–30 minutes (use the SYNC_KEY you chose in Step 2).
    3. That's it — it wakes the app and records new drives and charges on its own.

    🔋 A built-in overnight sleep window stops the syncing from waking your car while it rests, so it won't cause vampire battery drain.

  6. 6 📲 Install on your iPhone
    1. Open your Render address in Safari.
    2. Tap the Share button (square with an ↑ arrow).
    3. Choose Add to Home Screen → Add. It now launches full-screen from a ⚡ icon, like a native app, and opens even when offline.

        🌡️ Efficiency vs Temperature

        Average Wh/km grouped by the outside temperature during each drive (10°C bands). Shows how weather drives consumption — cold raises it (cabin/battery heating) and high heat raises it too (A/C). Each bar is the mean Wh/km of that band's drives.

        📈 Weekly Efficiency Trend

        Your average Wh/km per ISO week across the window — the mean of each week's drives. Reveals seasonal drift and whether your efficiency is improving over time.

        🛣️ Distance by Speed Band

        Total km driven grouped by each trip's average speed — City (<30), Urban (30–60), Rural (60–90), Highway (90+ km/h). Shows where your mileage happens; highway km cost the most energy per km (aerodynamic drag rises with speed²).

        🕒 Trips by Hour of Day

        Number of trips that started in each hour (0–23, Malaysia time). Reveals your commute rhythm and how much driving falls in peak-hour traffic.

        ⚡ Charging: AC vs DC Energy

        Share of total kWh added by AC (home / destination) vs DC (Supercharger / fast). DC is quick but adds heat and cell stress and usually costs more per kWh — a lower DC share is gentler on the pack and your wallet.

        🎯 Charge Target (End SoC)

        How often each end-of-charge battery level is used (rounded to 5%). Frequent charges to 100% accelerate degradation; keeping daily charges to 80–90% and only topping to 100% before long trips extends pack life.
